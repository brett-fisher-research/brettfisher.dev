I've struggled with perfectionism my entire life. I don't know why and I'm not going to try to diagnose this problem. I don't think that's very productive. Instead, I'm taking a new mindset that I have this moment, and I'll do my best with it.

## The Black Swan
I first read *The Black Swan* by Nassim Nicholas Taleb in high school and recently picked it up again. I couldn't put it down. I didn't totally understand what the point of
the book was when I first read it, but I realized that many of Taleb's points have subconsciously shaped many of the beliefs I've formed over the years since I first read the book.

Taleb's central argument is that what we don't know is more important than what we do know (or what we think we know). He illustrates this point with the metaphor of a black swan. Suppose you have only ever seen white swans in your life, and so you believe that all swans must be white. Every time you see a white swan, you become more convinced that all swans are indeed white. One day, however, you spot a black swan, and so you must admit that your assumption was wrong. You may have seen thousands of white swans, but it only took one black swan for you to realize that what you thought you knew was wrong.

Taleb argues that our world is governed by what he calls "black swan events", or rare events that we didn't think were possible but have a profound impact. For example, the
2008 global financial crisis was a black swan event. Every day before the crash was like spotting a white swan. It was easy to believe that since the market went up yesterday,
it probably will today too. Every day that the market went up, it became easier to believe that it would continue to do so, until the black swan event of the crash brought
financial ruin to a lot of people.

## Experts
A lot of people make their living by trying to predict the future. Go to any news site and you'll be flooded with articles of predictions from "experts" telling you what the
stock market is going to do today or where AI is going to take us. We love hearing predictions from experts because it gives us a sense of security, but we forget how many
predictions in the past have turned out to be completely wrong. 


The examples that come to mind are all the predictions about AI that surfaced when ChatGPT started gaining massive traction in 2023. How many "experts" predicted that within 6 months, software engineering jobs would be extinct? 3 years later, that hasn't happened. I'd even argue we need software engineers more than ever because of the opportunities
that AI-assisted coding has created.

You don't see news articles talking about failed predictions. Nobody holds experts accountable for when their predictions are wrong, and I'm not proposing any kind of solution to do so. I just don't think you should listen to anybody who claims to know the future in the first place. There are no such things as crystal balls.

## No right answers, only wrong ones
I quit my job 5 months ago to start my own business. The YouTube algorithm picked up on this really quickly, and soon I was being fed countless recommendations from "gurus" on what you need to do to succeed as an entrepreneur. For all the "expert" opinions out there, nobody really agreed on the same things. 

At first, this was discouraging. Was there any reliable source of information on what I should do? For all the answers I looked for, it only ever seemed like I ended up with more questions and uncertainty.

That's when I read *Going Infinite* by Michael Lewis about Sam Bankman-Fried and the collapse of FTX. Before starting FTX, Sam worked at a quantitative trading firm called Jane Street. Jane Street has a very unique interview process - instead of a typical interview where you sit down and are asked boring questions about your resume, Jane Street has candidates play strategic betting games with each other. In one such game, you are presented with a set of biased coins - some coins may have a 60% chance of landing on heads, or a 52% chance, but you don't know the odds for each coin. You will be paid $1 for every heads you flip and get 100 flips. Jane Street was interested in seeing what strategies you would use to maximize your profits.

What should you do? With only 100 flips, there's no way to find the "optimal" coin that has the highest chances of giving you a heads. Flipping coins at random isn't going to work out so well either. You're just going to have to find one or more coins that are "good enough" and keep flipping those to try to get as many heads as you can.
